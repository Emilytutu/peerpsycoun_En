“Three out of Six” Subject Interest Test

The fossilized system of arts and sciences division will be abolished in succession. Now many provinces have changed the college entrance examination policy to “three out of six” model of the new college entrance examination. Compared with the rigid division of arts and sciences in the past, this model is more humanistic and scientific.

For subject selection, grades are of great importance, which, to some extent, reflect your interest and strengths in the subject. However, for the vast majority of students, the three advantageous subjects may not be obvious, which requires a careful assessment of their interest in subjects. Because of the past education system, many people even don’t know what their interests are, or have no goals for themselves since childhood, which is definitely another serious problem in education. Schools and society do not provide children with the opportunity and platform to develop their own interests and hobbies. There’s nothing but grades and exams. As a result, when asked about future, people are bewildered and so are parents and teachers. We simply want to mend our ways so that we can rediscover who we really are. Below is a psychological test questionnaire of “three out of six” subject selection adapted from John Hollander’s LIBOS model that will hopefully be helpful.

SEE ALSO: John Holland is a professor of psychology at Johns Hopkins University and a famous vocational guidance specialist in the United States. In 1959, he put forward vocational interest theory with extensive social influence, holding that people’s personality types and interests are closely related to occupation, and interest is a great driving force for people’s activities. Any occupation with occupational interest can improve people’s enthusiasm and encourage people to actively and happily engage in the occupation. There is a high correlation between occupational interest and personality. If you are a student who has completed the college entrance examination and is waiting to choose a major, you can participate directly in the full version test of major selection via .

But, once allowed such an option, it’s easy for you to select "Don’t Matter". It’s all for passing the test anyway. If so, you won’t be able to discover your preferences.
